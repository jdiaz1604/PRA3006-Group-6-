About the project

We set out to test whether a country's socioeconomic profile (GDP and population size) correlates with the proportion of threatened endemic species. Our hypothesis: higher GDP and smaller population would mean a lower threatened/endemic proportion. After running linear regressions on Wikidata-sourced data, we did not find a significant correlation.

List of terms

Endemic species: a native species found only in a single geographic location (e.g., Australian Koala, Chinese Giant Panda).

GDP (Gross Domestic Product): total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country; used here as a proxy for economic health.

Correlation graph: a visual tool to show the relationship between two variables (positive, negative, or none).

What we did

  • Created SPARQL queries for species, GDP, and population.
  • Built an interactive map showing endemic counts and threatened categories (NT, VU, EN, CR) per country.
  • Ran regressions and plotted correlations for threatened fraction vs GDP and population.
  • Sharpened skills in GitHub, SPARQL, HTML, JavaScript, and CSS.

Limitations

  • Correlations are more complex than GDP and population alone.
  • Migrating species are underrepresented; endemic focus favors islands.
  • Only terrestrial species considered; marine species are excluded.
